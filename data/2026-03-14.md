### [1] [Language Model Teams as Distributed Systems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.12229)
*Elizabeth Mieczkowski,Katherine M. Collins,Ilia Sucholutsky,Natalia Vélez,Thomas L. Griffiths*

Main category: cs.MA

TL;DR: Using distributed systems principles to systematically design and evaluate LLM teams, revealing parallels between distributed computing challenges and LLM team dynamics.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: LLMs are becoming increasingly capable, driving interest in LLM teams, but there's a lack of principled framework for addressing key questions about team utility, optimal agent count, structural impacts on performance, and comparison with single agents. Current approaches rely on trial-and-error rather than theoretical foundations.

Method: Proposes treating LLM teams through the lens of distributed systems as a principled foundation for creation and evaluation. Rather than trial-and-error, the authors apply distributed systems concepts to analyze LLM team dynamics.

Result: Findings reveal that many fundamental advantages and challenges studied in distributed computing also arise in LLM teams, demonstrating rich practical insights from the intersection of these two research domains.

Conclusion: The cross-pollination between distributed computing and LLM teams provides valuable theoretical foundations for systematically addressing questions about when teams are helpful, how to structure them, and how they compare to single agents.

Abstract: Large language models (LLMs) are growing increasingly capable, prompting recent interest in LLM teams. Yet, despite increased deployment of LLM teams at scale, we lack a principled framework for addressing key questions such as when a team is helpful, how many agents to use, how structure impacts performance -- and whether a team is better than a single agent. Rather than designing and testing these possibilities through trial-and-error, we propose using distributed systems as a principled foundation for creating and evaluating LLM teams. We find that many of the fundamental advantages and challenges studied in distributed computing also arise in LLM teams, highlighting the rich practical insights that can come from the cross-talk of these two fields of study.
